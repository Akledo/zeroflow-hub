# Before You Buy: The Home Longevity Lab Electrical & Structural Checklist

The excitement of ordering a $6,000 infrared sauna or a $5,000 cold plunge often leads to a common prosumer oversight: **the installation logistics.** Unlike standard appliances, longevity hardware places unique demands on your home’s electrical and structural infrastructure.

At ZeroFlow, we’ve analyzed the failure points of hundreds of home installations. This checklist ensures your "Recovery Sanctuary" doesn't become a "Renovation Nightmare."

---

## 1. Electrical: Beyond the Standard Outlet
Most residential outlets in the US are 15-Amp circuits (NEMA 5-15R). While they work for a toaster, they are often insufficient for high-end longevity hardware.

### The 20A Requirement
Many 3-person infrared saunas and 1/2 HP cold plunge chillers draw over 1,800W. 
*   **The Problem:** On a 15A circuit, drawing more than 1,440W (80% of capacity) for an extended period can cause "nuisance tripping" or overheat the wiring.
*   **The Solution:** Install a **dedicated 20-Amp circuit** with a **NEMA 5-20R outlet** (identifiable by the "T-shaped" slot).
*   **The "Phantom Tripping" Trap:** High-end chillers have a high "inrush current" when the compressor starts. If your sauna and plunge are on the same circuit, the combined load will trip the breaker every time the chiller kicks in to maintain temperature. **Always use dedicated circuits for each major component.**

---

## 2. Structural: The "Floor Load" Math
A common misconception is that a cold plunge can go anywhere. However, water is heavy—extremely heavy.

*   **The Math:** 100 gallons of water weighs ~834 lbs. Add the weight of the tub (~150 lbs) and a 200 lb human, and you are looking at nearly **1,200 lbs** concentrated in a 10–15 sq ft area.
*   **The PSF Rating:** Standard residential floors are typically rated for 30–40 Pounds per Square Foot (PSF). A filled cold plunge can exceed **100 PSF**.
*   **ZeroFlow Recommendation:** If installing on a second floor or over a crawlspace, consult a structural engineer. Ground-floor concrete slabs (garages/patios) are the safest and most cost-effective locations.

---

## 3. HVAC & Ventilation: Managing the Heat Exchange
Chillers and saunas are thermal machines. They don't "create" cold; they move heat.

*   **Chiller Clearance:** A 1/2 HP chiller requires at least **12–18 inches of clearance** on all sides. Restricted airflow leads to "heat soaking," where the chiller pulls in its own hot exhaust, reducing efficiency by 30% and significantly increasing your electricity bill.
*   **Humidity Control:** Saunas (especially steam variants) and cold plunges (through evaporation) increase indoor humidity. Ensure your space has a high-CFM exhaust fan or a dehumidifier to prevent mold growth in wall cavities.

---

## 4. Water Logistics: Sanitation Over Chemicals
Traditional pool chemicals like high-dose chlorine are harsh on the skin and can damage the internal heat exchangers of high-end chillers.

*   **The ZeroFlow Stack:** We recommend a combination of **Ozone or UV-C sterilization** and a **5-micron sediment filter**.
*   **Scaling Prevention:** If you have "hard water," use a pre-filter when filling your plunge to prevent calcium buildup inside the chiller’s titanium heat exchanger.

---

## Final Verdict: Professional vs. DIY
While many cold plunges are marketed as "plug-and-play," the infrastructure they plug into is rarely ready. Budget an additional **$800–$1,500** for professional electrical and structural prep to protect your $10k investment.
