## Introduction
"Can I run my whole house on solar?" is one of the most common questions for homeowners looking to escape rising utility costs and achieve energy independence. The short answer is **yes**, but the technical reality involves much more than just slapping panels on your roof. 

To achieve 100% solar self-sufficiency, you must solve the "Physics of the Night"—the gap between when energy is produced and when it is consumed. In this guide, we break down the peak load math and battery buffer requirements needed to truly cut the cord.

## The Peak Load Reality Check
Most homeowners underestimate their peak power demand. While your average draw might be low, high-wattage appliances like HVAC systems, electric dryers, and well pumps create massive "spikes."

### Understanding Surge vs. Steady State
If your solar inverter is rated for 10kW but your heat pump draws 12kW during startup, your system will trip. Running a whole house requires an inverter stack capable of handling the simultaneous surge of your largest appliances.

## The Battery Buffer Math
Solar panels only work when the sun is shining, but your life doesn't stop at sunset. To run a house 24/7, your battery bank must be sized to cover your "Autonomy Days"—the number of days you can survive without significant solar generation (e.g., during a storm).

### Sizing for 24-Hour Autonomy
- **Minimalist Setup:** 10kWh - 15kWh (Lights, refrigeration, basic electronics).
- **Standard Home:** 30kWh - 50kWh (Adding climate control and laundry).
- **Prosumer/High-Demand:** 80kWh+ (Workshop tools, EV charging, multi-unit HVAC).

## Smart Management: The Missing Link
Running a house on solar isn't just about storage; it's about intelligence. A "dumb" house will drain its batteries by running a dishwasher at 10 PM. A "sovereign" house uses a Home Energy Management System (HEMS) to shift loads to peak production hours.

### Integrating HEMS for Efficiency
By using local control platforms like Home Assistant, you can automate your home to:
1. Pre-cool the house during peak solar hours.
2. Heat water when the battery is at 95% capacity.
3. Shed non-critical loads if the battery drops below a safety threshold.
